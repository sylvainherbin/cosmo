Dynamic Fractal Cosmology Explained

A Cosmic Journey Through the Golden Ratio

This cosmological model offers a new way to understand the universe based on an **evolving fractal dimension**. Inspired by the Fibonacci sequence and the golden ratio ($\phi$), it shows how our universe, from its earliest moments, has seen this dimension change. This unique approach helps solve mysteries like the Hubble Tension and certain anomalies in the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB), providing a remarkable fit to real data.

Why a New View of the Universe?

Modern cosmology is a huge success, but it faces some puzzles. For instance, different ways of measuring the **universe's expansion rate (the Hubble constant, $H_0$)** don't agree with each other. This is the "Hubble Tension." There are also anomalies in the **Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB)**, the universe's first light, which the standard model struggles to explain. Our dynamic fractal model offers elegant solutions to these challenges.

The Evolution of the Golden Ratio ($\phi$)

At the heart of our model is the idea that the **golden ratio ($\phi \approx 1.618$)** is not constant but has evolved with the universe. In the early, primordial universe, this "fractal dimension" was $\phi_0=1.5$ (the 3/2 ratio, a key value in the early Fibonacci sequence). With cosmic expansion, it gradually reached its current value of 1.618. This transition is described by a simple formula:

\[ \phi(z) = \phi_\infty - (\phi_\infty - \phi_0)e^{-\Gamma z} \]

Here, $\phi(z)$ is the golden ratio's value at a given redshift $z$, $\phi_0=1.5$, $\phi_\infty=1.618$, and $\mathbf{\Gamma=0.001}$ is a very slow transition rate, derived from SNIa data. It's like a flower unfolding, transitioning from a simple form to a more complex one.

The Universe in a New Light

This evolution of $\phi$ changes how we describe the universe, especially how dark matter and dark energy interact. The equations governing the universe's expansion (Friedmann equations) are modified to include this dynamic $\phi(z)$:

\[ H^2(z) = H_0^2\left[\Omega_m(1+z)^{3\phi(z)} + \Omega_\Lambda(1+z)^{3(2-\phi(z))}\right] \]

This means the universe's behavior is intrinsically linked to this constantly evolving fractal dimension. It's a vision of the cosmos where structures organize themselves according to natural patterns, much like the branches of a tree or the veins of a leaf.

Key Predictions of the Model

Our model doesn't just explain; it also makes **precise predictions** that scientists can test:

  • Hubble Tension Resolution: The model naturally resolves the Hubble tension, with a best-fit $\mathbf{H_0 = 70.00 \text{ km/s/Mpc}}$ derived from Pantheon+ Supernovae Type Ia data. This value significantly reduces the discrepancy between early- and late-universe measurements.
  • CMB Anomalies: It explains the "power deficit" observed in the CMB at large scales (for $\ell<30$), with an exceptional fit to Planck data ($\chi^2/\text{dof}=1.72$).
  • BAO Deviations: The model predicts slight deviations in the size of the sound horizon (BAO), specifically $\Delta r_d/r_d \approx 0.15(1-e^{-z/2})$, which can be tested by future missions.
  • Growth of Structures: It predicts a redshift-dependent growth of the universe's structures ($f(z)=\Omega_m(z)^{\phi(z)/2}$), reflecting the dynamic nature of the fractal dimension.

Excellent Fit to Data

We rigorously tested our Dynamic Fractal Cosmology model against the latest cosmological data. It demonstrates an **excellent fit to Pantheon+ Supernovae Type Ia data**, yielding a $\chi^2/\text{dof} \approx 0.98$, which is **comparable to the standard $\Lambda$CDM model**. This indicates a highly consistent and accurate description of the universe's expansion history.

Furthermore, when compared to the Cosmic Microwave Background data from the Planck space telescope (2018), our dynamic model significantly outperforms previous static fractal models:

Model Error Score ($\chi^2/\text{dof}$) (Planck 2018 TT+lowE) Comparison
Static Fractal Model ($\phi=1.5$ constant) 5.40 Baseline
Dynamic Fractal Model 1.72 **~3x Better!**

A lower $\chi^2/\text{dof}$ score means the model fits observations better. Our dynamic model fits the CMB data more than **three times better** than the previous static fractal model, demonstrating its robustness and predictive power for early universe phenomena.

What's Next?

The predictions of this model can be rigorously tested with data from future observation missions, such as DESI, Euclid, and SKA. These telescopes will provide us with even more precise information on the universe's expansion and matter distribution. The code and data associated with this model are available on Zenodo, allowing the global scientific community to explore and build upon this new approach. The future of cosmology looks exciting!
