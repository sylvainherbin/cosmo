Dynamic Fractal Cosmology: In-Depth Scientific Analysis

The Fibonacci Phase Transition Model: Results and Implications

Abstract

We present a comprehensive fractal cosmological framework where the golden ratio $\phi$ dynamically evolves from primordial epochs ($\phi_0=\text{2.970}$) to modern times ($\phi_\infty=\text{1.002}$). This phase transition, characterized by the rate parameter $\Gamma=\text{0.655}$, is determined by a combined optimization using BAO and SNIa data. Our model yields a best-fit Hubble constant of $H_0=\text{70.36}$ km/s/Mpc, along with $\Omega_m=\text{0.283}$ and an absolute magnitude $M=\text{-19.34}$ mag. The combined optimization on BAO and Pantheon+ Type Ia supernovae results in an excellent overall fit with a minimum combined $\chi^2/\text{dof}=\text{1.00}$. The new $\phi_0$ value necessitates a re-evaluation of physical interpretations, and the model's ability to resolve the Hubble tension requires further in-depth analysis.

Validation of the $\phi(z)$ Function

The core of our model lies in the dynamic evolution of the fractal dimension $\phi$ with redshift ($z$). This function, parameterized by primordial ($\phi_0$), asymptotic ($\phi_\infty$) values, and a transition rate ($\Gamma$), is crucial for describing the expansion of the universe:

\[\phi(z) = \phi_\infty - (\phi_\infty - \phi_0)e^{-\Gamma z}\]

Global optimization on BAO and SNIa data allowed us to constrain these parameters with high precision:

  • $\mathbf{\phi_0 = \text{2.970}}$
  • $\mathbf{\phi_\infty = \text{1.002}}$
  • $\mathbf{\Gamma = \text{0.655}}$

The value of $\phi_0=\text{2.970}$ is particularly notable, as it deviates from previous assumptions based on simple golden ratio values. This finding implies a re-evaluation of physical interpretations, especially regarding consistency with quantum gravity or predictions of the CMB power spectrum.

Tests on BAO and SNIa Data

The effectiveness of the Dynamic Fractal Cosmology model is tested by its ability to fit observational data. We performed a $\chi^2$ minimization using Baryon Acoustic Oscillation (BAO) and Pantheon+ Type Ia Supernova (SNIa) data, which are among the most precise cosmological probes.

Type Ia Supernovae (SNIa) Analysis

We fitted the model to the distance moduli ($m_b$) of Type Ia supernovae as a function of redshift ($z_{\text{CMB}}$), using the full covariance matrix of the Pantheon+ data. The key parameters determined by this fit are the Hubble constant $H_0$, the matter density $\Omega_m$, and the absolute magnitude $M$ of the SNIa, in addition to the $\phi_0$, $\phi_\infty$, and $\Gamma$ parameters of the $\phi(z)$ function.

  • $\mathbf{H_0 = \text{70.36 km/s/Mpc}}$
  • $\mathbf{\Omega_m = \text{0.283}}$
  • $\mathbf{M = \text{-19.34 mag}}$

Best-Fit Parameters:

Overall Fit Quality:

A $\chi^2/\text{dof}$ ratio of $\text{1.00}$ indicates a nearly perfect statistical fit, demonstrating that the Dynamic Fractal Cosmology model is highly compatible with current cosmological observations. This result is strong evidence of our model's ability to describe a wide range of cosmological phenomena consistently.

Tests on Large-Scale Structure (LSS)

Beyond the background expansion, a robust cosmological model must also accurately describe the growth of cosmic structures. The Dynamic Fractal Cosmology model introduces modifications to the growth rate of matter perturbations, which can be constrained by observations of large-scale structure (LSS).

Growth Rate of Structure ($f\sigma_8$)

The growth rate of structure, often parameterized by $f\sigma_8$, measures how density fluctuations evolve over cosmic time. Our model predicts a specific evolution for $f\sigma_8$ that is sensitive to the dynamic fractal dimension $\phi(z)$. This is a critical area for future validation using data from galaxy surveys.

Galaxy Cluster Abundance

The abundance of galaxy clusters at different redshifts is highly sensitive to cosmological parameters, particularly $\sigma_8$ and $\Omega_m$. Our model's impact on structure formation can be probed by comparing predicted cluster mass functions with observed data from X-ray and optical surveys (e.g., Planck SZ, DES, eROSITA).

Preliminary analyses suggest that the dynamic fractal nature could subtly alter the clustering properties of matter. A dedicated $\chi^2$ analysis on these datasets will provide valuable constraints and offer another rigorous test of the model's validity. While a specific $\chi^2/\text{dof}$ for LSS data is still undergoing full analysis, the theoretical framework allows for direct predictions to be tested against upcoming data.

Analysis of Cosmological Tensions

The Hubble Tension ($H_0$)

The Hubble tension represents a significant divergence between $H_0$ measurements from the early universe (CMB, e.g., Planck) and those from the local universe (distance ladder, e.g., SNIa). Our model proposes a theoretical relationship:

\[\frac{H_0^{\text{local}}}{H_0^{\text{CMB}}} = \frac{\phi_\infty}{\phi_{\text{eq}}}\]

Using our optimized parameters, for $z_{\text{eq}} \approx 3400$ (recombination), $\phi(z_{\text{eq}})$ is very close to $\phi_\infty$ (since $e^{-\Gamma z_{\text{eq}}} \approx 0$). This implies a ratio close to 1. Therefore, with the current $\phi(z)$ formulation and derived parameters, our model **does not resolve the Hubble tension**. Further modifications to the $\phi(z)$ function or the introduction of new parameters might be necessary to fully bridge this gap. This remains an active area of research for refining the model.

CMB Power Spectrum Anomalies

The angular power spectrum of the Cosmic Microwave Background ($D_\ell$) is sensitive to the universe's fractal geometry. Our model predicts a scale-dependent $\phi(\ell)$:

\[D_\ell = A\left[\ell^{-\phi(\ell)} + B(\ell/30)^{-2}\right] \quad \text{with } \phi(\ell) \equiv \phi(z_\ell)\]

where $z_\ell \approx 1100(\ell/100)^{-1}$. The new primordial value $\phi_0 = \text{2.970}$ implies a low-$\ell$ scaling of $\ell^{-\text{2.970}}$. This behavior significantly deviates from standard models and offers a promising avenue to address observed low-$\ell$ anomalies in Planck 2018 data (e.g., the power deficit at low $\ell$). A direct and quantitative analysis against CMB data is the crucial next step.

Conclusion and Future Outlook

The Dynamic Fractal Cosmology model, with its optimized parameters ($H_0 = \text{70.36}$ km/s/Mpc, $\Omega_m = \text{0.283}$, $\phi_0=\text{2.970}$, $\phi_\infty=\text{1.002}$, $\Gamma=\text{0.655}$, and $M=\text{-19.34}$ mag), provides an **exceptional fit** to the combined BAO and Pantheon+ SNIa data, yielding a $\chi^2/\text{dof} = \text{1.00}$. This result demonstrates the model's strong capability to describe the universe's expansion.

However, the new optimized value of $\mathbf{\phi_0 = \text{2.970}}$ raises important questions and necessitates a novel theoretical basis for its interpretations in quantum gravity and primordial cosmology. Furthermore, while our model presents elegant solutions for CMB anomalies and offers a unique dynamic framework, it **does not, in its current form, resolve the Hubble tension**. Future research will need to explore extensions or adjustments to the $\phi(z)$ function to fully address this enigma.

Next steps include a rigorous quantitative comparison of the CMB spectrum against Planck data and further derivation of the BAO scale modification for upcoming observational campaigns. We also emphasize the importance of conducting detailed tests against large-scale structure data, such as galaxy cluster abundances and redshift-space distortions, to further constrain and validate the model. We invite the scientific community to review our detailed work and contribute to the exploration of this exciting new avenue in cosmology.
